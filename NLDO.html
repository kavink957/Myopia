Nasolacrimal duct obstruction (NLDO)

Introduction

Congenital nasolacrimal duct obstruction (NLDO) is a common condition in infants characterized by a blockage or obstruction in the nasolacrimal duct, which is responsible for draining tears from the eyes into the nose. This blockage leads to the inability of tears to drain properly, resulting in excessive tearing, discharge, and sometimes infections. Congenital NLDO typically manifests within the first few weeks or months of life and can resolve spontaneously in many cases.

Causes

The exact cause of congenital NLDO is not always clear, but it is believed to be due to an obstruction or underdevelopment of the nasolacrimal duct system. Contributing factors may include:

Underdeveloped nasolacrimal duct:

The duct may be narrower or not fully formed, leading to inadequate drainage of tears.

Blocked tear duct openings:

Congenital NLDO can occur due to blockages or obstructions at various points along the nasolacrimal duct system.

Abnormalities in the structure of the duct:

Structural abnormalities or anomalies in the nasolacrimal duct can contribute to obstruction and impaired tear drainage.

Signs

Signs of congenital NLDO may include:

Excessive tearing (epiphora):

Persistent tearing or watery eyes, especially noticeable when the infant is feeding or outdoors.

Mucous or purulent discharge:

Discharge from the eyes, which may be clear, mucous-like, or occasionally purulent if secondary infection occurs.

Eyelid matting:

Crusting or stickiness of the eyelids due to dried tears and discharge.

Eyelid redness:

Redness or irritation of the eyelids, particularly along the inner corner of the eye (medial canthus).

Occasional eyelid swelling:

Mild swelling or puffiness of the eyelids, especially upon waking in the morning.

Symptoms

Common symptoms associated with congenital NLDO include:

Frequent blinking/ eye rubbing:

Infants may exhibit frequent blinking or rubbing of the eyes in response to discomfort or irritation caused by excessive tearing or discharge.

Eye irritation:

Irritation or redness of the eyes, particularly around the inner corner, due to constant moisture or contact with discharge.

Recurrent eye infections:

Secondary bacterial infections may occur due to the stagnant tears and debris in the blocked tear duct, leading to symptoms such as redness, swelling, and discharge.

Treatment

Treatment options for congenital NLDO may include:

Massage and warm compress:

Gentle massage and application of warm compresses to the tear duct area can help open the blocked duct and facilitate tear drainage.

Tear duct probing:

In cases where conservative measures are ineffective, a simple surgical procedure called tear duct probing may be performed to clear the obstruction.

Antibiotic eye drops:

If secondary infection occurs, antibiotic eye drops or ointments may be prescribed to treat the infection and reduce inflammation.

Observation:

In many cases, congenital NLDO resolves spontaneously within the first year of life without the need for intervention, so careful monitoring and observation may be recommended initially.

Management

Effective management of congenital NLDO involves:

Regular follow-up:

Monitoring the condition and assessing for resolution or progression, especially in infants who do not require immediate intervention.

Education and support:

roviding education and support to parents regarding proper eye hygiene, massage techniques, and signs of infection.

Prompt treatment of infections:

Prompt identification and treatment of any secondary eye infections to prevent complications and promote healing.

Surgical intervention:

If conservative measures are unsuccessful or if the condition persists beyond the first year of life, surgical intervention may be considered to clear the obstruction and restore proper tear drainage.
